HOW TO REPORT DOMESTIC VIOLENCE IN UGANDA

You have two options. Choose whichever feels safest:

OPTION 1 — GO TO THE POLICE
- Go to any police station. Ask specifically for the Child and Family Protection Unit (CFPU) or the Gender Desk.
- You can report in person OR call the GBV toll-free helpline: 0800 199 195 (free on MTN and Airtel).
- The police will record your statement, can arrest the abuser, refer you for medical care, and help you find shelter.
- Best for: immediate danger, physical or sexual abuse, urgent protection.
- Cost: FREE. You should not pay anything.

OPTION 2 — GO TO YOUR LOCAL COUNCIL COURT
- Go to your LC1, LC2, or LC3 court in the area where you or the abuser lives.
- The court will record your complaint and hold a hearing.
- Serious cases (repeat offenders, risk of further harm) are referred to police and magistrate's court.
- Best for: community-level disputes, first-time reporting, or when the police feel inaccessible.
- Cost: FREE.

WHAT THE POLICE MUST DO (Section 7 of the Act)
When you report, the police officer is LEGALLY REQUIRED to:
- Help you find shelter
- Ensure you get medical care if there are signs of physical or sexual abuse
- Tell you your legal rights
- Offer guidance and assistance for your wellbeing

HOW TO GET A PROTECTION ORDER

A protection order is a court order that legally stops the abuser from contacting or harming you. Breaching it is a criminal offence.

STEP 1: Go to the nearest magistrate's court, family and children court, or local council court.
STEP 2: Ask for Form 1 (Application for Protection Order) — a standard form under the Act.
STEP 3: Fill in the form and attach an affidavit (a written sworn statement describing what happened).
STEP 4: Submit the form. A police officer or representative can also do this on your behalf.
STEP 5: If you are in immediate danger, ask for an INTERIM (emergency) order the same day. The court can issue this even without the abuser being present.

WHAT A PROTECTION ORDER CAN DO:
- Order the abuser to stop all abusive behaviour
- Order the abuser to leave the shared home
- Order the abuser to stay away from your home, workplace, and children's school
- Order the abuser to have no contact with you (no calls, texts, or in-person contact)
- Give you temporary custody of children
- Order the abuser to pay maintenance

A full protection order stays in force until the court changes or cancels it. Breaking a protection order carries the same penalties as domestic violence itself — up to 2 years in prison.
