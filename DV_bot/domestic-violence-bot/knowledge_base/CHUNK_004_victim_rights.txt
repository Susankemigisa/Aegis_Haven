YOUR RIGHTS AS A VICTIM OF DOMESTIC VIOLENCE IN UGANDA

RIGHT TO REPORT — FREE OF CHARGE
You have the right to report domestic violence to the police OR to your local council court. You can also send a trusted representative to report on your behalf. You should NOT be asked to pay any money to make a report.

RIGHT TO HAVE YOUR STATEMENT TAKEN BY AN OFFICER OF THE SAME SEX
If you go to the police, you have the right to request that a police officer of the same sex as you takes your statement. (Section 7(4) of the Act)

RIGHT TO FREE MEDICAL HELP
If you show signs of physical or sexual abuse, the police are legally required to ensure you receive a medical examination and treatment. (Section 7(2)(b))

RIGHT TO A PROTECTION ORDER
You have the right to apply for a court order (protection order) that legally stops the abuser from harming or contacting you. You, a representative, or a police officer can apply on your behalf. Courts can issue emergency (interim) orders the same day if you are in immediate danger.

RIGHT TO COMPENSATION
The court can order the abuser to pay you financial compensation for the harm they caused. The amount is decided by the judge based on your pain and suffering, the nature of the violence, and any medical or other costs you incurred. (Section 4(3))

RIGHT TO SHELTER ADVICE
When you report to the police, they are legally required to advise you and help you find shelter if needed. (Section 7(2)(a))

RIGHT TO KNOW YOUR OPTIONS
Police must inform you of your right to apply for legal relief and your right to file a criminal complaint. (Section 7(2)(c))

IMPORTANT: Your consent to the violence is NOT a legal defence for the abuser. (Section 5)

COMMON MYTH: "It is a family matter, so nothing can be done." — This is FALSE. The law applies inside families. Domestic violence is a criminal offence regardless of the relationship.
