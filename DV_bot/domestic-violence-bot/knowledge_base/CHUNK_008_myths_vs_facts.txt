COMMON MYTHS ABOUT DOMESTIC VIOLENCE IN UGANDA — AND THE TRUTH

MYTH: "Domestic violence is a family matter. Courts and police should not get involved."
FACT: This is FALSE. Uganda's Domestic Violence Act, 2010 makes domestic violence a criminal offence — regardless of whether it happens inside a family. Police and courts are legally required to act on complaints.

MYTH: "I agreed to stay so nothing can be done."
FACT: This is FALSE. Under Section 5 of the Act, the victim's consent is NOT a legal defence. Even if you did not resist, the abuser can still be charged and convicted.

MYTH: "I have to pay the police to take my report."
FACT: This is FALSE. You should NEVER be asked to pay money to file a domestic violence complaint. Reporting is free. If an officer demands money, ask for a senior officer or call the CFPU helpline: 0800 199 195.

MYTH: "Only women can be victims of domestic violence."
FACT: This is FALSE. The law protects all genders. Men can also be victims and are entitled to the same protections.

MYTH: "I need a lawyer to report domestic violence."
FACT: This is FALSE. You can report directly to the police or local council court without a lawyer. Free legal help is available if you need it from FIDA Uganda (0800 111 511) or Justice Centres Uganda (0800 100 210).

MYTH: "I cannot get a protection order without the abuser being present."
FACT: This is FALSE. Courts can issue an INTERIM (emergency) protection order without the abuser being present (ex parte), if the court believes you are in immediate danger.

MYTH: "If I report, I will be forced to leave my home."
FACT: A protection order can actually order the ABUSER to leave the home — not you. The court can direct the perpetrator to vacate the shared home while the order is in force.

MYTH: "Domestic violence only means physical hitting."
FACT: This is FALSE. Ugandan law recognises physical, sexual, emotional, psychological, verbal, and economic abuse — plus harassment and intimidation — all as forms of domestic violence.
