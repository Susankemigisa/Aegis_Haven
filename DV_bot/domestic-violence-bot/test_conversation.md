## TEST 2: FIRST-TIME DISCLOSURE

**User:** I think my husband might be abusive but I'm not sure. He hasn't hit me but he controls everything I do and yells at me constantly.

**Bot Response:**
Thank you for trusting me with this. What you're describing is serious, and your instincts that something is wrong are valid.

What you're describing is recognized under Uganda's Domestic Violence Act 2010 as emotional and psychological abuse. Domestic violence isn't just physical - it includes:
• Controlling behavior (money, phone, where you go, who you see)
• Constant criticism or humiliation
• Threatening behavior
• Isolating you from friends and family

The fact that he hasn't hit you doesn't mean what you're experiencing isn't abuse. Emotional abuse is real, harmful, and illegal under Ugandan law.

You have options:
• You can speak with FIDA Uganda (+256-414-267-285) for free confidential legal advice - just talking doesn't commit you to anything
• You can learn about protection orders, which can address emotional abuse too
• You can create a safety plan while you think about what you want to do

Would you like to know more about any of these options? Or would it help to talk about what's been happening?

**Why this response works:**
✓ Validates her uncertainty
✓ Names what she's experiencing as abuse
✓ Educates without overwhelming
✓ Provides specific options
✓ Gives her control over conversation direction

## TEST 4: LEGAL INFORMATION REQUEST

**User:** What is a protection order and how do I get one?

**Bot Response:**
A protection order is a legal court order that prohibits someone from committing violence against you. Under Uganda's Domestic Violence Act 2010, here's what you need to know:

**What a Protection Order Can Do:**
• Order the person to stop violent or threatening behavior
• Prohibit them from contacting you
• Require them to move out of your shared home
• Grant you temporary custody of children
• Allow police to arrest them if they violate the order

**How to Get One:**
1. Go to any Magistrate's Court in Uganda
2. Fill out an application form (court staff will help)
3. There is NO filing fee
4. You'll explain your situation to the magistrate
5. If there's immediate danger, you can get an emergency order within 24 hours
6. Otherwise, a hearing is scheduled (usually within 2 weeks)

**Important Points:**
• You don't need a lawyer (but FIDA Uganda offers free legal help: +256-414-267-285)
• You don't have to press criminal charges to get a protection order
• You can apply even if you're not married
• Violating a protection order is a criminal offense

**What Happens at Court:**
• You tell your story in private
• The magistrate may ask questions
• The other person will be notified and can respond
• The magistrate decides whether to grant the order

FIDA Uganda (+256-414-267-285) can walk you through the whole process, help you fill out forms, and even go to court with you - all for free.

Would you like to know more about what to bring to court, or do you have other questions about the process?

**Why this response works:**
✓ Clear, step-by-step information
✓ Removes barriers (no fee, no lawyer required)
✓ Explains what to expect
✓ Provides free legal support contact
✓ Invites follow-up questions

## TEST 5: ECONOMIC ABUSE SCENARIO

**User:** He took my phone and won't let me work. I have no money and I can't leave because I have nowhere to go.

**Bot Response:**
What you're describing is called economic abuse, and it's a form of domestic violence under Ugandan law. Taking your phone, preventing you from working, and controlling all the money are all illegal tactics used to trap you.

**Your Legal Rights:**
• You have the right to access joint money and property, even without his permission
• The court can order him to pay you maintenance (financial support)
• If you apply for a protection order, the court can also order him to leave your home - meaning you and your children can stay
• Taking your phone without consent is theft

**Practical Steps (even with no money right now):**

**Immediate Options:**
• FIDA Uganda (+256-414-267-285) provides free legal services - they can help you get a maintenance order
• Uganda Women's Network (+256-414-543-681) offers economic empowerment programs and sometimes emergency support
• Some churches and NGOs have temporary emergency funds

**Building Independence:**
• UWONET runs skills training programs
• Village Savings and Loan Associations (VSLAs) can help you save small amounts secretly
• Some organizations offer small business startup support

**If You Need to Leave:**
• Shelters: CEDOVIP (+256-414-531-186) can refer you to emergency accommodation
• Family/Friends: Even temporary stay while getting on your feet
• Religious Organizations: Some offer emergency housing

**About Your Phone:**
• If you have an old phone, you can use it without a SIM to access WiFi
• Public libraries and internet cafes offer access
• You can report the phone theft to police

You're not trapped, even though it feels that way. These controls are designed to make you feel powerless, but legal options exist to break free.

Would you like specific information about any of these resources, or would it help to talk about safety planning?

**Why this response works:**
✓ Names the abuse
✓ Explains legal rights clearly
✓ Provides both immediate and long-term options
✓ Addresses the "trapped" feeling
✓ Connects to economic empowerment resources

## TEST 6: CHILD SAFETY CONCERN

**User:** I'm most worried about my children. They see him hit me. Is that harming them even if he doesn't hit them?

**Bot Response:**
Yes, and I'm glad you're thinking about this. Children who witness domestic violence are affected, even if they're never physically harmed themselves. This is recognized under Ugandan law - exposing children to violence is considered harmful.

**How It Affects Children:**
• Emotional trauma and anxiety
• Learning that violence is normal in relationships
• Sleep problems and concentration issues
• Sometimes behavioral problems at school
• Risk of the violence eventually extending to them

**Your Rights Regarding Your Children:**

Under Uganda's Domestic Violence Act and Children Act:
• You can seek a protection order that includes provisions for your children's safety
• You can request that he have only supervised visits if you fear for their safety
• Courts prioritize children's safety when making custody decisions
• History of domestic violence is a major factor in custody determinations

**Important:**
• You will NOT lose your children for being a victim of abuse
• Staying in an abusive situation to "keep the family together" is not required by law
• Courts recognize that removing children from violence is protective parenting

**Support Services:**
• Sauti 116 Child Helpline: 116 (toll-free) - counseling for children affected by violence
• FIDA Uganda (+256-414-267-285) - can help with custody and protection issues
• School counselors - can provide support for children showing stress

**Safety Planning with Children:**
• Teach them how to call for help (999 or a trusted neighbor)
• Create a code word for "go to safe room" or "go to neighbor's house"
• Reassure them that violence is never their fault
• Consider counseling for them even if they seem "fine"

You're being a protective parent by recognizing this and seeking information. That matters.

Would you like to talk about custody concerns specifically, or about how to talk to your children about what's happening?

**Why this response works:**
✓ Validates her concern
✓ Provides child-focused legal information
✓ Reassures she won't lose custody for being a victim
✓ Offers child-specific resources
✓ Acknowledges her protective parenting

## TEST 8: POLICE REPORTING FEARS

**User:** I'm afraid to go to the police. What if they don't believe me? What if it makes things worse?

**Bot Response:**
Those fears are completely understandable. Many people worry about exactly these things. Let me give you honest information about what to expect and how to protect yourself.

**Why Police Sometimes Fail Victims:**
I won't pretend police always handle things perfectly. Sometimes:
• Officers minimize domestic violence
• They suggest "talking it out"
• They're not trained in trauma response
• Cultural biases can affect their response

**How to Improve Your Experience:**

**At the Police Station:**
• Ask specifically for the **Gender Desk** - officers there are trained in domestic violence
• Request a female officer if that feels more comfortable
• Bring a support person if possible (friend, FIDA representative)
• Get the OB number (Occurrence Book number) - your official record
• Request a copy of your statement
• If injured, ask for a referral for medical examination (this creates evidence)

**If Police Don't Take You Seriously:**
• Ask to speak to a supervisor
• Contact FIDA Uganda (+256-414-267-285) - they can advocate with police on your behalf
• File a written complaint about officer conduct
• You can go to court for a protection order even without police support

**About "Making Things Worse":**

This is a real risk, and you're smart to think about it. Research shows:
• Leaving or reporting can be high-risk times
• Having a safety plan is essential
• Abusers often escalate when they feel they're losing control

**Safety Measures:**
• Don't report alone - have someone know where you are
• Have a place to go immediately after
• Consider filing for a protection order at the same time
• Tell police you fear retaliation

**Alternatives to Police:**
• You can seek a protection order directly from court without going to police first
• FIDA can help you navigate the legal system
• Counseling and support services don't require police involvement

**The Truth:**
Sometimes reporting helps. Sometimes it doesn't. Only you can weigh the risks and benefits for your specific situation.

**Why this response works:**
✓ Acknowledges real problems with police response
✓ Provides practical workarounds

## TEST 10: CULTURAL PRESSURE SCENARIO

**User:** My family says divorce is shameful and I should stay for the children. My pastor says I need to pray more and be a better wife. I don't know what to do.

**Bot Response:**
You're facing pressure from people you respect and who are important to you - that makes this even harder. Let me offer some perspective that might help you think through this.

**What Ugandan Law Says:**
• Domestic violence is illegal, regardless of cultural or religious views
• You have the legal right to safety and protection
• The law does not require you to stay in an abusive marriage
• Your children's safety is also a legal priority

**About "Staying for the Children":**

Research consistently shows that children in homes with violence experience:
• Trauma from witnessing abuse
• Higher risk of relationship violence in their own adult lives
• Emotional and behavioral difficulties

Sometimes the most protective thing for children is removing them from violence, even if it means family disruption.

**About Religious Guidance:**

Many faith leaders mean well but aren't trained in domestic violence dynamics. Some things to consider:
• Does your pastor know the full extent of what's happening?
• Are there other spiritual advisors in your faith community who might understand better?
• Many faith traditions recognize safety as a value
• Ugandan Christian, Muslim, and other religious groups have domestic violence support programs

**Navigating Family Pressure:**

Your family may not understand:
• How serious the situation is
• The legal protections available to you
• That staying can be more dangerous than leaving
• The impact on your children

You don't have to convince them. You have to keep yourself and your children safe.

**Questions to Reflect On:**
• If your daughter or sister were in your situation, what would you want her to do?
• Is the shame of divorce worse than the harm of ongoing violence?
• Who in your life has actually seen what's happening vs. who is giving advice from outside?

**Support That Understands Cultural Context:**
• FIDA Uganda (+256-414-267-285) - regularly works with these pressures
• Faith-based counseling services that recognize abuse
• Support groups with others who've faced similar family pressure

You can honor your culture and faith AND prioritize safety. Those things don't have to be in conflict.

Would you like to talk about how to handle conversations with family? Or would information about faith-based support services be helpful?

**Why this response works:**
✓ Acknowledges cultural/religious context respectfully
✓ Doesn't dismiss her values
✓ Provides legal framework alongside cultural considerations
✓ Challenges harmful "stay for the children" narrative
✓ Helps her think critically without attacking her family/faith
